Why you should stop using for loops on arrays…. or almost.




Note: this bill was written for JavaScript beginners. If you do not recognize yourself as a beginner, spare this reading for yourself and move on.

F or loops are great.

In fact, they’re not just great. They’re terrific. After all, if your teacher ordered you to write down “I won’t be a bully anymore” 1000 times, you would only wish you had some kind of these loops mounted in your writing hand so you’re able to seamlessly finish the task. And the paper on your desk.

Arrays are amazing as well. Stocking whatever data you want in an infinite space of letters and numbers separated by commas, jailed between two square braces, only fearing a memory leak that would never happen (unless you wanna stock all the data about all the history of mankind in Earth, you’re gonna be okay) . Lining up the data you want, in the order you want, structured and organized in one of the most flexible basic structures of programming in general, and JavaScript in particular. How sweet.

Combine these two things, and you get a for loop running on an array (whoa!). In fact, these loops do not specifically run on arrays, but whenever a solution on any problem includes “multiple times” and “arrays”, we are more tempted than ever to use the shortest, closest-to-earth solution, which is :
* declaring a placeholder variable (most of the time, it would hold the name “placeholder”, one of the most creative names one could use)
* running a traditional for loop with our traditional “pointer” variable called i
* doing whatever we need to do with the array, using that magical “i” variable
* manipulate the output value we need and affect it to “placeholder”
* return placeholder.

But here’s the thing. Doing this is bad. It’s not like “Robbing is bad” bad, but more like “You shouldn’t be doing that bad stuff anymore, grow up!” bad. It’s ugly. Hard to read, hard for your brain to allow it to synthetize what you just did, and as such, harder to understand, harder to maintain, and harder to debug. And easier to send to trash. You will be pardoned and your life will be spared when you’re actually dealing with a low-level language (such as C), but today, in JavaScript, you simply can’t do this anymore, without having a big bright LED sign reading “NEWBIE HERE” pointing at you if you don’t care to move up the ladder. And trust me, the earlier you do, the better it will be. Much better. Like “black Friday pricing” better.

Don’t get me wrong. I used to do this as well. It was all birds chirping and sky being blue and cloudless until I stopped for a try into a website called “CodeWars”. It’s basically a website that, for a specific language of your choice (of course, amongst the available ones on the website), offers a series of challenges that you must pass. In order to pass them, you are given your assignment, and your code will be tested against a sample of several test cases, which should all pass in order to continue. And because you’re basically handling arrays most of the time, all what I was doing is brainlessly writing for loops, tailoring and tinkering and messing with endless if/else if/else clauses just because one test case failed. And of course, when it worked, I was so happy. And then I stated to myself “I LOVE for loops”…. until I realized almost immediately that my approach would be wrong.

After you pass a challenge, you are allowed to see what others have done in order to pass it, ranked by how many “upvotes” they have, and how many people consider the way they used as a “best practice”. And trust me, these “best practices” would shock you, had you not read this post, before trying to make your own way to this beautiful yet somewhat intimidating website. And also, it’s up since a good while, so expect a “best practice” to only receive more upvotes and best practice mentions as random wayfarers pass by, as well as you shouldn’t expect your answer to receive a vote or two.

Back to our story, everytime I would take a sneak peek at these solutions, I would always be between surprised and shocked. Techniques that required no loops, that were 90% smaller than my code or approach, but damn effective and functional. The legendary word trio W-T-F was accompanying every attempt of deciphering and thinking how in the world did this guy make it happen, only to discard what I just saw and stubbornly continue with my wrong-yet-working approach. Until I finally started discovering these beautiful methods at Mozilla Developer Network’s related pages, and it was breathtaking — at that moment — how wonderful things can be with a well-sought line or 2.

Of course, I would not just “read for the sake of reading”. I would have a “sandbox.html” file loaded in my favorite browser, while editing a “sandbox.js” file in my favorite text editor, all of this only to grasp, little by little, the magic of object-oriented programming, when it applies to arrays. Mashing F5 and looking out for what the console printed out, whether it was an error or the dreamed output that I was wishing to have, was never as fun as back in these days.

With my duty of making you scroll across all of what could be called “nonsense” accomplished, it’s finally time to make you follow me and help you getting rid of your habit of abusing loops.

Wanna run some sort of transformation from an array to another one, like doubling or tripling numbers? Use Array.prototype.map.

Do you need a new array that contains the elements that comply with some sort of logic that you provide (like “odd numbers only”)? Array.prototype.filter. Looking for some value in any given array? Array.prototype.includes (beware, this method was only available with ES6 / ES2015, and as such is only applicable in modern browsers, aka the ones you should be using in 2018: Firefox, Chrome, and at a lesser extent, Opera ). Turn your array into a string? Array.prototype.join. Flip your array upside… I mean revert your array’s content? Array.prototype.reverse. Some other Array methods don’t require for loops to be reproductible, and as such they won’t be covered here.

By the way, I’m listing all these methods by beginning with “Array.prototype.whatever”, but you just call them straight away like “Array.map” or so. Check the code example below for more. And check out the result at the end. This is why, people, I wrote what you’re reading right now.

Don’t worry, they’re all built-in, standardized methods. No need to invent them out of nothing. Really.

You might come across Array.prototype.forEach. But forEach is the one example of why using a for loop over an array method is justified. The one and only. And this counter-example to the whole point of this post would slightly make the “Don’t use for loops at all” feeling become a little less significant, even though I didn’t mention word by word that you should completely get rid of this way to do stuff. In fact, one reason forEach should be discarded, is for performance issues, the only kind of issues that really matters, when it comes to dealing with big software and programs.

Of course, for the sake of brevity and simplicity, I’ve only named the methods that you would definitely need and use in an everyday fashion.
